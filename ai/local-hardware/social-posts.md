## LinkedIn

**Why CAPIX is moving its large AI models from the cloud to local hardware**

For several years, we've run CAPIX's AI cashflow forecasting and risk models on cloud infrastructure. It served us well while we developed and validated our hybrid forecasting models and autonomous treasury agents.

But as those models have grown larger — and more central to how our customers manage liquidity — we've been re-evaluating where that compute should live. Increasingly, the answer is **on local hardware, in-house.**

At the centre of this strategy is the **NVIDIA DGX Spark** — a compact AI supercomputer built on the GB10 Grace Blackwell platform that brings data-centre-class capability to the desk.

Four reasons the shift makes sense for treasury workloads:

🔒 **Data sovereignty** — Treasury data (bank balances, payment instructions, counterparty exposures) is among the most sensitive information a business holds. Processing it on hardware we own means it never has to leave a controlled environment to reach a third-party AI service.

💷 **Cost predictability** — For continuous, round-the-clock training and inference, metered cloud GPU costs add up fast. Owning the hardware converts an unpredictable operating expense into a known, fixed cost.

⚡ **Efficiency & latency** — Keeping the model, the data and the application close together removes network round-trips, enabling faster reforecasting and tighter iteration.

🛠️ **Independence** — We control the full stack, from the silicon to the model weights to the treasury application that consumes them.

This isn't a move away from the cloud altogether. We see a **hybrid future**: local hardware for the heavy, sustained and data-sensitive workloads, complemented by cloud where elastic scale genuinely adds value.

The goal is simple — put each workload where it runs best, and deliver AI-driven treasury tools that are faster, more cost-efficient, and built with data sovereignty designed in from the start.
